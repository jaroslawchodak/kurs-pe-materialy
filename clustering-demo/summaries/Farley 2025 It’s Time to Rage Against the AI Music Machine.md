### MAIN ARGUMENT
The article argues that AI-generated music threatens the core values of the music industry — particularly authenticity and meaningful creative struggle — and calls for the industry to distinguish AI products from human artists by quarantining them on separate charts and ensuring proper compensation mechanisms. Farley contends that without guardrails, AI music will not only displace human musicians economically but will also degrade musical culture by training humans to "sound more like AI," creating a "downward spiral into slop" and trapping audiences in "a ceaseless remix of the past" {Farley, 2025 #115990}

### KEY CLAIMS
AI-powered musical acts are already charting: Xania Monet became the first known AI-powered act to debut on a Billboard airplay chart, and Breaking Rust topped the "Country Digital Song Sales" chart. Billboard reports that at least six AI or AI-assisted acts have appeared on its charts in recent months, though the actual number may be higher because it is difficult to identify which acts use AI and to what extent {Farley, 2025 #115990}

The music industry may favor AI over human artists for managerial convenience: AI never requires rehab, doesn't trash hotel rooms, doesn't renegotiate contracts, and "just pumps out products" — making it an attractive alternative to "notoriously difficult musician personalities" for some industry managers {Farley, 2025 #115990}

Public perception research shows listeners cannot distinguish AI from human music: a recent Ipsos survey for French streaming service Deezer found that 97% of people polled could not tell if a song was created by a human or generated by AI. This perceptual indistinguishability poses a threat to human musicians' market position {Farley, 2025 #115990}

AI dominance will create a cultural feedback loop in which humans imitate machines: Because music is a "trend-driven industry" where "artists, fans and music executives chase after what's hot," if AI music dominates the charts, "humans will begin to echo the machines," resulting in a "downward spiral into slop." This reverses the traditional direction of creative influence {Farley, 2025 #115990}

AI undermines authenticity, a core value of popular music: Pop music controversies center on whether artists are "keeping it real" — as seen in debates around Beyoncé's country credibility, the Kendrick Lamar/Drake authenticity battle, and Kurt Cobain's assertion that "The worst crime I can think of would be to rip people off by faking it." AI is "the ultimate faker" because it has no lived experience — it hasn't "earned the right to sing the blues — or jazz, country, rock, and hip-hop" {Farley, 2025 #115990}

Industry stakeholders are beginning to take sides: iHeartMedia Chief Programming Officer Tom Poleman sent a memo pledging not to play "AI music that features synthetic vocalists pretending to be human," citing research showing 96% of consumers find "Guaranteed Human" content appealing. Over 1,000 musicians including Paul McCartney contributed to a protest album titled "Is This What We Want?" (released December 8 on vinyl) aimed at AI music that fails to properly compensate human artists {Farley, 2025 #115990}

### EXPERT VOICES
**Prince (musician)** — in a 1999 interview, refused to allow tape recording of his voice, expressing distrust of what "future technology might do with unauthorized recordings." Farley retrospectively characterizes this as prescient rather than paranoid {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Tom Poleman, iHeartMedia (Chief Programming Officer)** — issued internal memo declaring company policy not to play AI music with synthetic vocalists pretending to be human, stating "Sometimes you have to pick a side — we're on the side of humans" and citing research that 96% of consumers find guaranteed human content appealing {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Nick Cave (singer-songwriter)** — in a letter to fans, argued that creativity is meaningful because it requires effort, contrasting AI services like ChatGPT that "churn out entertainment products with no experience, no effort, and no rest" and therefore produce output with no meaning. Wrote: "ChatGPT rejects any notions of creative struggle, that our endeavours animate and nurture our lives giving them depth and meaning" {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Ralph Ellison (writer)** — quoted from *Living with Music* defining the blues as "an impulse to keep the painful details and episodes of a brutal experience alive in one's aching consciousness, to finger its jagged grain, and to transcend it, not by the consolation of philosophy but by squeezing from it a near-tragic, near-comic lyricism." Farley uses this to argue AI lacks the life experience necessary to authentically perform blues or related genres {Farley, 2025 #115990}

### CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
**AI artist / AI product distinction** — Farley proposes the recording industry stop referring to AI music releases as "artists," which "anthropomorphizes AI in a way that's unearned." Instead, they should be called "AI products" to maintain a categorical distinction from human musicians with "real lives" and "real financial obligations" {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Downward spiral into slop** — the predicted cultural degradation resulting from humans imitating AI-generated music once it dominates the charts, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of derivative, inauthentic output {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Hot AI Products Chart** — Farley's proposed solution: quarantining AI-generated music on a dedicated Billboard chart separate from human musicians, analogous to existing genre-specific charts {Farley, 2025 #115990}

**Guaranteed Human content** — term used by iHeartMedia to describe music verifiably created by human artists; positioned as a market differentiator in response to consumer research showing 96% find such content appealing {Farley, 2025 #115990}

### RELEVANCE FOR RESEARCH
This article documents real-time industry responses to AI music at a moment of rapid technological change, providing named examples of AI acts that have charted (useful for empirical tracking) and institutional policy positions (iHeartMedia's human-only policy). The Ipsos/Deezer survey data point (97% cannot distinguish AI from human music) is a concrete empirical claim researchers can follow up on or contextualize. Farley's framing of the issue through authenticity discourse offers a cultural analysis angle — the invocation of Prince, Ellison, Cave, Cobain, and Beyoncé situates AI music within longstanding debates about realness in popular music. The proposed "Hot AI Products Chart" is a policy intervention worth tracking for implementation. The article also exemplifies how mainstream media frames AI creativity as an existential threat to human culture, invoking both economic displacement (musicians' livelihoods) and aesthetic degradation (the "ceaseless remix of the past"). The protest album "Is This What We Want?" with 1,000+ musicians represents organized resistance worth monitoring. For researchers studying AI and cultural production, platform governance, or authenticity politics, this serves as a primary source capturing stakeholder positions and the discursive framing of AI music in 2025 {Farley, 2025 #115990}
