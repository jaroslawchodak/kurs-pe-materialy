### MAIN ARGUMENT
The article reviews Justin R. Garcia's book *The Intimate Animal* to argue that modern humans are experiencing an "intimacy crisis" — a historically unprecedented disconnect from evolutionarily ingrained drives toward pair-bonding, connection, and reproduction. Through a synthesis of evolutionary biology, online dating research, and contemporary cultural trends (looksmaxxing influencers, AI companions, heteropessimism), the piece contends that digital technologies, economic structures, and generalized anxiety are interfering with millions of years of evolved social instincts, leaving unprecedented numbers of people unpartnered, lonely, and turning to profit-driven substitutes for human intimacy {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

### KEY CLAIMS
More than 40 percent of American adults — up to 120 million people — are currently unpartnered, a figure that has grown in recent decades alongside declining marriage rates. East Asian countries such as Japan and South Korea are witnessing similar patterns. This rise in singlehood has been accompanied by widespread loneliness, yet "something about modern life is interfering with millions of years of evolutionarily honed instincts toward human connection" {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

Garcia traces pair-bonding in humans to approximately 4.4 million years ago, emerging alongside bipedalism, which enabled face-to-face sexual intercourse and transformed sex into "an expression of intimate connection." Socially monogamous pair bonds were evolutionarily adaptive because they allowed mothers to recover from birthing vulnerable, large-brained infants while fathers invested in offspring care, enabling overlapping child-rearing cycles. The agricultural revolution and the internet represent "the two greatest changes to human courtship in the last 4 million years" {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

Nearly four out of ten relationships now begin online, yet billions of daily swipes on dating apps yield an average match rate of less than 2 percent. Research by University of Michigan sociologist Elizabeth Bruch shows that people tend to swipe right on profiles that are, on average, 25 percent more desirable than themselves. Garcia argues that the human brain is not equipped to process the massive volume of options presented online, and that app design emphasizing physical attractiveness contradicts research showing that "kindness" is the trait people most want in a partner {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

The article attributes part of the intimacy crisis to online influencer culture and gender dynamics amplified by social media. Male influencers like Clavicular (a looksmaxxing figure who considers sex "a waste of time" and has likely rendered himself infertile through testosterone-replacement therapy) and Andrew Tate may have shifted norms by treating women dismissively, while women respond with growing online mistrust of men, circulating "man or bear" memes and trends like "boysober," "decentering men," and "heteropessimism" {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

Garcia proposes that humans are responding to "generalized anxiety" — ongoing extreme uncertainty rather than isolated stressors. Research shows that small environmental stressors (winter storms, localized tragedies like the Oklahoma City bombing) induce baby boomlets, but chronic uncertainty causes animals to "turn inward, conserve resources, and cool it on having offspring." Instead of forming adaptive pair bonds, people are increasingly turning to profit-driven substitutes: sugaring, sex work, pornography, and AI companions. AI companions are being marketed as solutions to "touch starvation," with proponents touting tactile VR technologies, despite the evolutionary precedent being human-to-human connection {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

### CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
**Intimacy crisis** — Garcia's term for the current historical moment in which unprecedented numbers of adults are unpartnered and lonely despite evolutionarily honed drives toward connection and pair-bonding {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

**Looksmaxxing** — a nihilistic pursuit of physical attractiveness or "sexual market value" divorced from actual pursuit of relationships or sex; exemplified by influencer Braden Peters (Clavicular), who considers sex "a waste of time" and has likely compromised his fertility through testosterone therapy {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

**Aspirational dating** — Garcia's term for the tendency to pursue romantic prospects who are significantly more desirable than oneself, a strategy that may work in small dating pools but becomes dysfunctional when users can "swipe 3,000 people before lunch" {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

**Heteropessimism** — a broader cultural trend (referenced alongside specific practices like going "boysober" or "decentering men") in which women express growing mistrust of men, possibly in reaction to #MeToo and online misogyny {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

**Generalized anxiety** — Perel's term for the chronic, extreme uncertainty characterizing contemporary life, as opposed to isolated environmental stressors. In animal studies, chronic uncertainty causes organisms to conserve resources and reduce reproduction, whereas acute stressors can trigger baby boomlets {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

**Touch starvation** — a condition associated with increased loneliness and lack of physical contact; cited by AI-companion advocates as justification for developing tactile VR technologies to simulate human touch {Sussman, 2026 #119050}

### RELEVANCE FOR RESEARCH
This article is valuable as a synthesis document that connects evolutionary biology (Garcia's academic field) with emerging digital phenomena that are not yet extensively studied in peer-reviewed literature: looksmaxxing culture, heteropessimist discourse, AI companionship as a loneliness intervention, and dating-app psychology. It provides concrete data points (40% of U.S. adults unpartnered; <2% match rate on dating apps; people swipe on profiles 25% more desirable than themselves; nearly 40% of relationships now begin online) and names specific institutional sources (Kinsey Institute, Match's "Singles in America" survey, Elizabeth Bruch's research). The article is also useful for discourse analysis: it documents how mainstream media frames the "intimacy crisis" as both an evolutionary anomaly and a consequence of platform capitalism. Researchers studying digital intimacy, platform design, loneliness interventions, or the political economy of AI companionship may find the expert attributions and framing devices valuable as empirical anchoring points. The article explicitly raises the question of financial incentives misaligned with human connection ("no one profits from that hug"), making it relevant for critical studies of technology and commodification {Sussman, 2026 #119050}
