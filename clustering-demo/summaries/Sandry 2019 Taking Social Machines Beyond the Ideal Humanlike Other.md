### MAIN ARGUMENT
The chapter argues that the prevailing design philosophy of making communicative machines (robots and bots) as humanlike as possible is fundamentally limiting and potentially harmful. This drive toward "ideal" humanlike communication narrows the design space for social machines, undermines recognition of diverse human and non-human communication styles, and marginalizes people who do not communicate in idealized humanlike ways themselves. As an alternative, Sandry proposes embracing machines as "overtly non-human others" — absolute alterities that can engage meaningfully in social networks without mimicking human communication, thereby opening up new possibilities for understanding selfhood, sociality, and communication across human and non-human actors in both physical and digital spaces {Sandry, 2019 #192562@69–70}.

### KEY CLAIMS
The standard definitions of "social robot" and "socialbot" presuppose that being social requires being humanlike in communication and behavior. Breazeal defines a sociable robot as one that is "socially intelligent in a humanlike way" such that "interacting with it is like interacting with another person," while early socialbot developers describe them as software that can "pass itself off as a human being" on social network sites. This conflation of humanlike-ness with sociality pervades the field, shaping design priorities and evaluation criteria, even though this assumption limits what counts as meaningful social interaction with machines {Sandry, 2019 #192562@71–72}.

Non-humanlike bots demonstrate that meaningful social interaction does not require passing as human. Twitter bots like @GardensBritish, @MagicRealismBot, and @tinycarebot openly declare their bot status and communicate in obviously non-humanlike ways — through algorithmically generated surreal text, random GIF responses, or standardized wellness reminders — yet successfully occupy social roles within users' Twitter networks. Similarly, Japanese character bots prioritize "the capacity of non-human action in online social media" over human mimicry. These bots become valued network members by capitalizing on their otherness rather than hiding it, proving that bots can be social precisely because they communicate differently from humans {Sandry, 2019 #192562@73–75}.

Physically instantiated robots can also function socially without humanlike communication, as demonstrated by two contrasting examples. Vyo, designed as a smart-home interface inspired by microscope morphology, communicates primarily through non-verbal embodied movements (head bowing, "nervous breathing," "urgent panic" gestures) and requires physical manipulation of "phicons" rather than voice or touchscreen interaction. Despite its non-anthropomorphic design and minimal vocal communication, Vyo successfully conveys emotion and maintains social presence while remaining "unobtrusive" most of the time. This demonstrates that social robots can balance engagement with unobtrusiveness and communicate meaningfully through alternative modalities {Sandry, 2019 #192562@75–78}.

The theoretical distinction between "evocative objects" (which "invite projection" without demanding engagement) and "relational artifacts" (which "demand engagement") offers insight into different modes of machine sociality. Twitter bots and Vyo function as evocative objects — communicative but non-demanding, revealing themselves gradually without compelling humanlike reciprocal responses. In contrast, socialbots designed to pass as human and robots like Jibo function as relational artifacts, constantly seeking attention and engagement in ways that invite comparison with human communication standards and risk being judged deficient. Positioning machines as evocative objects rather than relational artifacts grants them freedom to communicate in novel non-human ways while reducing the pressure to meet human ideals {Sandry, 2019 #192562@78–79}.

Extending Levinas's concept of "the face" to human-machine encounters provides a philosophical framework for valuing machine alterity without requiring humanlike reciprocity. In Levinas's thought, the "face to face" encounter is fundamentally asymmetrical — the self must respond to the other's revealed "face" (their personality or being as expressed through communication and behavior) without expecting equal reciprocal action. Applied to machines, this framework allows bots and robots to communicate and respond "in their own particular ways, with no need for reciprocal action — which also means, no need to be overtly humanlike." This philosophical position preserves the "absolute alterity of the other" even as self and other enter into proximity, supporting respect for machine communication on its own non-human terms {Sandry, 2019 #192562@79–80}.

### CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
**Robot** — physically instantiated machines that sense and respond to the world around them in at least seemingly intelligent ways; may be humanlike (Hanson/Ishiguro creations), animal-like (Aibo, Paro), or overtly machinelike (industrial robots, floor cleaners) {Sandry, 2019 #192562@70}.

**Bot (software robot)** — pieces of computer code that operate as entities capable of responding to events in digital spaces, most commonly interacting with humans and other bots through online social platforms and websites; may be programmed to pass as human or designed to be recognizably non-human {Sandry, 2019 #192562@71}.

**Social robot** — according to Breazeal's influential definition, a robot that "is socially intelligent in a humanlike way" such that "interacting with it is like interacting with another person"; this definition conflates sociality with humanlike-ness {Sandry, 2019 #192562@71}.

**Socialbot** — according to early developers, software that automatically controls a social network site account and "is able to pass itself off as a human being" during interactions with other users; the structured interface of SNSs supports this passing {Sandry, 2019 #192562@71}.

**Performative classification** — N/A

**Evocative object** — objects (including Twitter bots and robots like Vyo) that "invite projection" without demanding engagement; reveal themselves gradually through communication and behavior, encouraging anthropomorphism without compelling humanlike reciprocal responses {Sandry, 2019 #192562@78}.

**Relational artifact** — objects (including socialbots and robots like Jibo) that "demand engagement" from humans; positioned as conversational agents whose seemingly humanlike nature requires treatment comparable to human interaction standards, risking judgment as deficient when compared to human ideals {Sandry, 2019 #192562@78–79}.

**The face / face to face** — Levinas's concept extended to machines: the "face" is not a physical face but a transcendent property encapsulating all ways an other expresses their personality or being; the "face to face" encounter is fundamentally asymmetrical, requiring the self to respond to the other without expecting reciprocal action on equal terms {Sandry, 2019 #192562@79–80}.

**Absolute alterity** — the condition of being radically different rather than lesser (or greater) than; a way of positioning non-human machines in networks that does not valorize ideal human communication but rather recognizes machines as others with their own non-human modes of being and communicating {Sandry, 2019 #192562@70}.

### CONNECTIONS TO OTHER CHAPTERS
The chapter explicitly situates itself as part of the 2017 volume "A Networked Self," noting that Nikolaos Mavridis contributed to the original 2011 volume where he observed "disembodied or even physically embodied intelligent software agents" beginning to appear on social network sites. Sandry builds on Mavridis's earlier discussion of Sarah, a robot with Facebook connectivity designed to sustain relationships through shared memories and social grooming. The chapter positions itself as extending the networked self framework to question how machines might participate in networks without conforming to human ideals, thus expanding earlier discussions of technology's role in constituting networked identities {Sandry, 2019 #192562@69–72}.

### CHAPTER ROLE
Develops a critical theoretical alternative to dominant assumptions about human-machine sociality within the "networked self" framework, challenging the field to recognize non-humanlike communication as valuable and expanding what counts as social in human-machine networks.
