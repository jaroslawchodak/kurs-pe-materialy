### MAIN ARGUMENT
The article reports on a new paper published in *Science* by 22 international experts predicting that advances in artificial intelligence will enable a fundamental transformation in disinformation campaigns: from labor-intensive operations like Russia's Internet Research Agency (2016) to autonomous "AI swarms" — thousands of social media accounts controlled by a single operator, capable of mimicking human behavior, evolving in real time, and targeting specific communities with unprecedented precision. The authors argue that these systems pose an existential threat to democracy because they can manipulate beliefs and behaviors at population scale while remaining virtually undetectable with current monitoring tools, and that urgent preventive action is required before the 2028 U.S. presidential election {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

### KEY CLAIMS
The 2016 Internet Research Agency operation in St. Petersburg employed hundreds of workers manually posting on social media to influence the U.S. election, but its impact was minimal compared to other Russia-linked actions such as the leaked Clinton emails. Current disinformation detection systems were designed for this labor-intensive model and are now obsolete as AI technology enables a qualitative leap in capability {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

The *Science* paper predicts that one person with AI tools will soon be able to command "swarms" of thousands of social media accounts that craft unique posts indistinguishable from human content, maintain persistent identities and memory, coordinate to achieve shared objectives while creating individual personas to avoid detection, and adapt in real time to platform signals and human interactions — all without constant human oversight {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

These AI swarms will be capable of mapping social networks at scale, allowing operators to position agents for maximum impact and tailor messages to the specific beliefs and cultural cues of each targeted community, enabling far more precise targeting than previous botnets. The systems could run millions of micro-A/B tests, propagate winning variants at machine speed, and iterate far faster than humans, essentially becoming self-improving through response feedback {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

The researchers state it is unclear whether such swarms are already deployed because current systems for tracking coordinated inauthentic behavior cannot detect them. They predict these systems are "definitely possible" and "likely being tested" with some human oversight, may not have massive impact on the 2026 U.S. midterms but will "very likely be deployed to disrupt the 2028 presidential election" {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

The paper proposes establishing an "AI Influence Observatory" composed of academic and NGO researchers to standardize evidence, improve situational awareness, and enable faster collective response. Notably, social media platform executives are excluded because platforms incentivize engagement over everything else and therefore have little incentive to identify swarms — revealing AI-driven engagement would threaten their business model, while concealing it inflates their valuation {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

### EXPERT VOICES
**Lukasz Olejnik, King's College London Department of War Studies (Visiting Senior Research Fellow, author of *Propaganda: From Disinformation and Influence to Operations and Information Warfare*)** — argues that targeting chosen individuals or communities will become "much easier and powerful" with AI swarms, characterizing this as "an extremely challenging environment for a democratic society" and stating "we're in big trouble" {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**Barry O'Sullivan, University College Cork School of Computer Science and IT (Professor)** — confirms that "AI-enabled influence campaigns are certainly within the current state of advancement of the technology" and agrees the paper identifies significant complexity for governance measures and defense response, despite being generally optimistic about AI's potential {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**Jonas Kunst, BI Norwegian Business School (Professor of Communication, coauthor of the *Science* paper)** — states "we are moving into a new phase of informational warfare on social media platforms where technological advancements have made the classic bot approach outdated." Notes detection is extremely difficult because swarms can mimic humans and researchers lack platform access due to increasing restrictions. Predicts swarms are "definitely possible" technically, "likely being tested," and will "very likely be deployed to disrupt the 2028 presidential election" {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**Nina Jankowicz, American Sunlight Project (CEO, former Biden administration disinformation czar)** — characterizes the paper's scenario as "Russian troll farms on steroids" and warns that "what's scariest about this future is that there's very little political will to address the harms AI creates, meaning [AI swarms] may soon be reality" {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

### CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
**AI swarms** — coordinated systems of thousands of AI-controlled social media accounts commanded by a single operator, capable of maintaining persistent identities and memory, simulating believable online personas, coordinating to achieve shared objectives while creating individual output to avoid detection, and adapting in real time to platform signals and human conversation {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**Coordinated inauthentic behavior** — the existing framework used by platforms and researchers to detect organized manipulation campaigns on social media; the article implies current detection systems are based on identifying patterns from labor-intensive operations and cannot identify AI-driven swarms {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**AI Influence Observatory** — proposed institutional response consisting of academic and NGO researchers (explicitly excluding platform executives) tasked with standardizing evidence of AI-driven influence campaigns, improving situational awareness, and enabling faster collective response rather than imposing top-down penalties {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

**Micro-A/B tests at machine speed** — the capability of AI swarms to conduct millions of simultaneous experiments testing different message variants, propagate the most effective versions, and iterate optimization cycles far faster than human-operated campaigns, creating self-improving disinformation systems {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}

### RELEVANCE FOR RESEARCH
This article documents expert consensus (22 international researchers publishing in *Science*) on an emerging sociotechnical phenomenon that represents a qualitative shift in information warfare capabilities — moving from detectable, labor-intensive operations to potentially undetectable, autonomous systems. The article is valuable for several reasons: it provides specific institutional affiliations for quoted experts, enabling academic follow-up; it captures the framing of AI agents as an existential threat to democracy in mainstream tech journalism; it documents the predicted timeline (2028 as critical inflection point) for empirical tracking; and it reveals structural obstacles to governance (platform business model conflicts, lack of political will, researcher access restrictions). The concept of "AI swarms" offers researchers a term for cross-referencing emerging literature on AI agents, coordinated inauthentic behavior, and computational propaganda. The article also illustrates how public discourse is constructing analogies between current AI capabilities and historical disinformation operations (IRA as baseline), which may be useful for discourse analysis or framing studies {Gilbert, 2026 #146943}
