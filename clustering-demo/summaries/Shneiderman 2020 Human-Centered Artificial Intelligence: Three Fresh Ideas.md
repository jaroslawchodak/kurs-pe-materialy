- Authors: Ben Shneiderman
- Year: 2020

### RESEARCH PROBLEM
The paper addresses the widespread belief that AI-driven human-like robots will inevitably take over human roles, replacing rather than augmenting human capabilities. Shneiderman challenges the dominant paradigm that positions humans as subordinate to or 'in-the-loop' around AI systems, arguing that this framing leads to out-of-control technologies, fears of robot-driven unemployment, and reduced user control over privacy and security. The author identifies a tension between two AI goals: emulating human behavior (AI science) and developing useful applications (AI engineering), arguing that designers go astray when lessons from the first goal are inappropriately applied to the second {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@110}.

The commentary seeks to provide an alternative conceptual framework that shifts focus from algorithm-centric to human-centric design, aiming to create technologies that amplify rather than replace human abilities. This reframing is motivated by concerns about current AI discourse, which emphasizes machine autonomy, algorithm performance, and what AI systems can do, rather than how they serve human needs and enhance human capabilities {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@112}.

### THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
The paper draws on Lewis Mumford's (1934) concept of 'The Obstacle of Animism' from *Technics and Civilization*, which describes how new technology designers mistakenly use humans or animals as design templates. Mumford's historical perspective shows that successful technologies evolve away from mimicking biological forms—exemplified by the shift from two-legged transport to four-wheeled vehicles—and that 'the most ineffective kind of machine is the realistic mechanical imitation of a man or other animal' {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

Shneiderman employs a Copernican Revolution metaphor to describe the HCAI paradigm shift: just as Copernicus reframed astronomy by placing the sun rather than Earth at the center of the solar system, HCAI reframes AI by placing humans rather than algorithms at the center of system design. This 'Second Copernican Revolution' reverses the traditional view that humans are 'in-the-loop' around AI, instead positioning AI as 'in-the-loop' around humans {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@112}.

The framework also engages with psychological research on human-computer interaction, particularly work by Clifford Nass and colleagues (Reeves and Nass, 1996; Nass and Moon, 2000) demonstrating that users respond socially to human-like computer designs. However, Shneiderman critiques what he terms 'Nass's Fallacy'—the belief that because people respond socially to robots, robots should be designed to be social or human-like—arguing instead for 'Shneiderman's Conjecture' that successful robots utilize distinctive machine features through well-designed user interfaces that avoid human-like characteristics {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

### KEY FINDINGS
The paper presents a two-dimensional HCAI framework that breaks from the traditional one-dimensional view (originally proposed by Sheridan and Verplank, 1978) that more automation necessarily means less human control. The new framework separates human control (y-axis) from computer automation (x-axis), demonstrating that high levels of both are simultaneously achievable. Examples include thermostats, elevators, self-cleaning ovens, dishwashers, highly automated cars, patient-controlled pain relief devices, and smartphone cameras, where AI algorithms handle technical operations (focus, aperture, shake compensation) while users maintain control over creative decisions (framing, timing, zoom, filters, editing) {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@115}.

The framework identifies four quadrants: high automation with low human control (airbag deployment, embedded pacemakers), high human control with low automation (piano playing, bicycling), low levels of both (morphine drip bag), and the optimal HCAI quadrant of high automation AND high human control (patient-controlled analgesia systems with clinician monitoring). The model also recognizes dangers of excessive automation (Boeing 737 MAX crashes, flash crashes, biased parole/hiring algorithms) and excessive human control (drunk drivers, suicidal pilots), emphasizing the need for well-designed interlocks {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@115}.

Shneiderman proposes four design tradeoffs where combined approaches are superior to either extreme: (1) Intelligent Agent versus Powerful Tool, (2) Simulated Teammate versus Tele-Operated Device, (3) Autonomous System versus Supervisory Control, and (4) Humanoid Robot versus Mechanical-like Appliance. For each pair, the author argues for hybrid designs that leverage machine capabilities while maintaining human control. For instance, rescue robots are more effective when they send continuous visual data (video showing location, temperature, air quality, obstacles, routes) rather than attempting natural language status reports, and when they use four wheels or treads rather than two legs to mimic human form {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@116}.

The paper introduces a three-level governance structure comprising 15 specific recommendations organized across three domains: (1) Reliable systems through software engineering team practices (audit trails, verification/validation testing, bias testing, explainable user interfaces), (2) Safety culture through organizational management strategies (leadership commitment, safety-oriented hiring/training, failure reporting, internal review boards, industry alignment), and (3) Trustworthy certification through independent oversight (government regulation, external audits by accounting firms, insurance compensation, NGO involvement, professional standards). This structure bridges the gap between widely discussed ethical principles and practical implementation steps {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@118}.

The author argues that shifting terminology, imagery, and metaphors away from intelligent autonomous teammates toward powerful tools and tele-operated devices will reduce fears of existential AI threats while increasing user confidence in maintaining control over technology. This linguistic and conceptual reframing supports the broader goal of designing systems that enhance human self-efficacy, creativity, and responsibility rather than replacing human judgment {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@117}.

### CONCEPTS AND DEFINITIONS
**Human-Centered AI (HCAI)** — a design approach that puts humans at the center of systems design thinking, enabling people to see, think, create, and act in extraordinary ways by combining potent user experiences with embedded AI methods, while ensuring human control, supporting self-efficacy, clarifying responsibility, and facilitating social participation {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@110}.

**Second Copernican Revolution** — a metaphorical reframing of AI design that reverses the traditional view of humans as 'in-the-loop' around AI systems, instead positioning AI as 'in-the-loop' around humans, analogous to how Copernicus shifted astronomy from an earth-centered to sun-centered model {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@112}.

**The Obstacle of Animism** — Lewis Mumford's (1934) concept describing the tendency of new technology designers to use humans or animals as design templates, which historically has limited technological innovation; exemplified by Mumford's observation that 'the most ineffective kind of machine is the realistic mechanical imitation of a man or other animal' {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

**Nass's Fallacy** — the mistaken belief that because people respond socially to robots, it is appropriate and desirable to design robots to be social or human-like {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

**Shneiderman's Conjecture** — the proposition that successful robots utilize distinctive machine features and will become more tool-like, tele-operated, and under human supervisory control through well-designed user interfaces that avoid human-like features {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

**Teammate Fallacy** — the mistaken assumption that because humans work in teams, computers should be designed to have the same behaviors as human teammates {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

**Computers-in-the-Loop Reality** — the recognition that while humans work with others in teams, crews, and groups, computers are best designed as helpful tools that continuously provide information and carry out tasks under human control, rather than as teammates {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

**Two-dimensional HCAI framework** — a model that separates human control (y-axis) from computer automation (x-axis), demonstrating that high levels of both are simultaneously achievable, in contrast to traditional one-dimensional models suggesting an inevitable tradeoff between automation and control {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@115}.

**Information abundant displays** — visual interfaces that present extensive information in compact, spatially stable, tiled layouts enabling users to maintain situational awareness, interpret status, formulate plans, and monitor performance, often superior to ephemeral spoken responses; exemplified by NASA spacecraft control rooms, air traffic control centers, and COVID dashboards {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@114}.

**Safety culture** — an organizational management approach characterized by leadership commitment to safety, hiring and training oriented to safety, extensive reporting of failures and near misses, internal review boards, and alignment with industry standard practices {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@119}.

### CONNECTIONS AND IMPLICATIONS
The author argues that HCAI principles can guide designers toward addressing vital technical goals including privacy, security, fairness, reliability, safety, and trustworthiness, while supporting broader societal objectives such as environmental protection, social justice, and human rights. Shneiderman explicitly connects HCAI to the 17 United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, arguing that attaining these goals requires combining technology developments with behavioral changes to improve healthcare and wellness, end poverty and hunger, protect the environment, and advance quality education, gender equality, global peace, and social justice {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@120}.

The paper acknowledges that promoting HCAI ideas will not be easy due to entrenched beliefs about human-like AI abilities held by researchers, developers, journalists, and policy makers. The author notes that changing these attitudes and expectations could take a generation, but suggests that framing HCAI as a Second Copernican Revolution could help accelerate adoption of combined approaches that create reliable, safe, and trustworthy applications {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@117}.

Shneiderman builds on existing ethical frameworks for responsible AI (citing Fjeld et al., 2020; IEEE, 2019) but argues these principles alone are insufficient—bridging the gap between ethics and practice requires realistic management processes. The three-level governance structure is proposed as a practical implementation framework that moves beyond abstract principles to concrete organizational practices {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@120}.

The author positions HCAI as complementing rather than opposing AI research goals, stating that 'the HCAI approach advances the goals of AI, while ensuring human control.' This framing attempts to reconcile the science goal of emulating human behavior with the engineering goal of developing useful applications, while cautioning against inappropriately applying lessons from the former to the latter {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@111}.

The paper draws on historical examples of successful technology evolution that moved away from animistic designs: smiling simulated bank teller machines gave way to form fill-in ATMs, and intelligent tutoring machines with human faces evolved into MOOCs with well-designed user interfaces. Similarly, humanoid rescue robots with two legs gave way to more reliable four-wheeled or treaded devices and aerial drones that function as tele-operated appliances (citing Murphy, 2014) {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@113}.

The author acknowledges that confusion exists regarding the boundary between automation and autonomy, noting that while some researchers believe autonomous systems based on machine learning enable greater adaptability and intelligence compared to traditional automated systems, others see no clear boundary and emphasize dangers of unpredictability. Shneiderman addresses both automated and autonomous designs within the same framework, recognizing that traditional automated systems are increasingly incorporating autonomous features based on AI algorithms {Shneiderman, 2020 #253839@112}.
